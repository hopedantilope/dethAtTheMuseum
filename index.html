Heart Exhibition

Living hearts beat for those already silenced. The present was always meant to be the hereafter. We remember better than we see - and we shall gladly remember for you.

Anatomy – What are the heart's parts?

The heart is an organ that is part of the cardiovascular system, which also includes arteries and veins. The heart is connected to both the pulmonary and systemic blood circulation. In humans, the heart is divided into four chambers: the upper left and right atria and the lower left and right ventricles. The heart also consists of valves, which in a healthy heart ensure one-directional blood flow and prevent backflow. Another form of protection for the heart is the pericardium, which encloses the entire organ. The wall of the heart is composed of three layers: the epicardium, myocardium, and endocardium.

Physiology – How your heart works?

The heart is connected to the pulmonary and systemic circulation systems, both of which have different but equally important functions. The main function of the heart is to pump oxygenated blood throughout the body and deliver not only oxygen, but also nutrients and hormones.

Pulmonary Circulation – How and why does the blood flow to the lungs from the heart?

The pulmonary circulation is the connection between the heart and the lungs. In humans, deoxygenated blood from the entire body enters the heart through the superior and inferior vena cava and flows into the right atrium. From the right atrium, the deoxygenated blood is pumped into the right ventricle and then sent through the pulmonary circulation to the lungs. In the lungs, the blood receives oxygen and, in its oxygenated form, flows back to the heart into the left atrium. From this point onward, the blood enters the systemic circulation.

Systematic Circulation - How and why does the blood flow to the whole body from the heart?

Oxygenated blood from the lungs enters the heart through the left atrium and is then pumped into the left ventricle. The left ventricle generates high blood pressure, allowing the blood to be pumped through the aorta to the entire body. The function of oxygenated blood is to flow through arteries and arterioles and deliver oxygen to all parts of the body. Oxygen is carried by red blood cells through the hemoglobin molecule. In the tissues, oxygen is delivered to the cells, while carbon dioxide moves from the tissues into the blood, causing the blood to become deoxygenated again. The deoxygenated blood then flows back to the heart through venules and veins, which connect to the superior and inferior vena cava, and enters the heart through the right atrium. This cycle repeats continuously.

Cardiac conduction system - What is the principle of your heart beating constantly?

The rhythm of the heartbeat, whether it is about 60 beats per minute at rest or up to 130 beats per minute during physical activity, is regulated by electrical signals within the heart. The signal is generated by the sinoatrial node, which is made of the pacemaker cells and therefore it is called the heart's pacemaker. These cells produce an electrical impulse, which travels through the electrical cardiac conduction system of the heart, causing it to contract and pump the blood to the whole body.

Heart disease

The heart is part of the cardiovascular system, which includes the heart, arteries, veins, and capillaries. Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are a group of diseases, which affect the heart and blood vessels. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are the leading cause of death worldwide. CVDs account for an estimated 19.8 million deaths each year (data from 2022). More than four out of five CVD-related deaths are caused by heart attacks and strokes, and one third of these deaths occur prematurely in people under 70 years of age. Besides the CVDs heart can also suffer from other diseases including: heart valve disorder (failure of a valve to close completely), arrhythmias (irregular rhythms), heart failure (poor pumping), congenital heart defect.

Heart attacks (Myocardial infarction)

The heart muscle itself is nourished by oxygenated blood supplied through the coronary arteries. If blood flow in one of these arteries decreases significantly or stops completely, it can cause an infarction (tissue death) of the heart muscle.

The first phase occurs when the coronary arteries become damaged due to various risk factors. These risk factors include high blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, lack of physical activity, obesity, high blood cholesterol, poor diet, and excessive alcohol intake. Over time, these factors damage the coronary arteries.

In the next phase, the body attempts to repair the damaged arteries. Blood cell fragments called platelets attach to the damaged area of the artery and may clump together to form blood clots. If a clot becomes large enough, it can partially or completely block blood flow through a coronary artery and cause a heart attack.

Symptoms of heart attack

Symptoms of heart attack can include pain or discomfort in the centre of the chest and/or in the arms, left shoulder, elbows, jaw or back. Moreover, the person may experience difficulty in breathing or shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, light-headedness, faintness, a cold sweat and turning pale. Women are more likely than men to have shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, and back or jaw pain. People experiencing these symptoms should seek medical care immediately.

Prevention of disease – What can you do to reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease?

It has been shown that not using tobacco, avoiding harmful alcohol consumption, reducing salt intake, eating more fruits and vegetables, and maintaining regular physical activity can reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease. It is also important to keep in mind that cardiovascular diseases are influenced by environmental and hereditary factors as well.

The Heart That Was Never Truly Understood

I am just the heart, but it doesn’t beat anymore. I used to smoke, but I don’t smoke anymore. They told me—just as I now tell you—that smoking eventually ends you. Like making that red, beating heart fall silent.

How much does each cigarette shorten a life? Twenty minutes. I started smoking at fifteen and quit at fifty-five. That’s forty years of cigarettes. How many did I smoke each day? Say twenty. Twenty times twenty: four hundred minutes a day. In that sense, I shortened my life by six and a half hours, every day.

I died later, of a heart attack, at sixty. Even after five years smoke-free.

Was it worth it? No way. And at the same time: smoking isn’t just smoking, you know? What made me smoke? What did it mean to me?

Listen. I think we live in a society where who and what you’re not matters more than what you are. I don’t smoke. I don’t eat candy on weekdays. I’m not someone who doesn’t donate organs. I don’t skip the bus fare. I’m not boisterous. I don’t swallow gum. I don’t leave food on my plate even when I’m full. I am not.

Not. Not. Not.

So how hard was my heart really beating while it was still beating? Maybe smoking was the last beat in a sea of no-beat. I am a smoker. Not someone who doesn’t not smoke, but a smoker. That thought makes me smile—sadly, but still.

Why did I quit at fifty-five? I have, in a way, always surrendered. I have always been a body with parts others can’t get enough of. I surrendered to the plumbing trade. To the place my wife wanted to live.And now I have surrendered my organs to you. At last, I even surrendered smoking.

The transition isn’t that great, if you know what I mean.

Still: I am not a person who doesn’t donate his organs.

It’s strange, isn’t it?

M 014

A 60-year-old man. He works as a plumber. His father died from heart disease, and his brother has angina. He used to smoke but stopped 5 years ago. He was admitted to the hospital because doctors thought he might be having a heart attack. He woke up during the night with strong chest pain behind the breastbone. After being admitted, he developed fluid in the lungs. Later, his heart went into a dangerous rhythm (ventricular fibrillation) and then stopped beating (asystole).

Nervous System Exhibition

The brain is the command center of the human body—a universe of connections housed within the skull. Every thought, every memory, every heartbeat begins here. In this exhibit, we invite you to explore the organ that makes us who we are.

Anatomy – What are the brain's parts?

The human brain weighs on average about 1.3 kg. The largest part of the brain is the cerebrum, which consists of two cerebral hemispheres (left and right). Each hemisphere is divided into four lobes: the frontal lobe, parietal lobe, temporal lobe, and occipital lobe. The surface of the brain is folded into ridges (gyri) and grooves (sulci).

Another part of the brain is the brainstem, which resembles a stalk. The brainstem includes the midbrain, the pons, and the medulla oblongata. Behind the brainstem is the cerebellum, which can be translated from Latin as "little brain." The brain is further covered by three membranes and the skull, which protect it from injury. The brain is connected to the body via the spinal cord and cranial nerves. [source]

Physiology – What are the main functions of your brain?

The brain has many functions, including processing sensory input, controlling movement, storing information, making decisions, and regulating emotions. It receives information from the sense organs (eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and skin) and interprets this information and guides behavior and responses.

Additionally, the brain contains specialized areas for language, such as Broca's area and Wernicke's area, which are responsible for language production and comprehension. [source]

Brain diseases

The brain can suffer from different types of diseases with different origins. Brain diseases include neurodegenerative diseases, which are caused by a progressive loss of neurons (Alzheimer's disease, dementia, Parkinson's disease). Vascular diseases are caused by a limited supply of blood flow and include ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke.

Epilepsy and headache disorders are caused by dysregulation of electrical activity. Brain infections are caused by bacteria or viruses (meningitis, encephalitis). Developmental and genetic disorders are caused by problems in brain formation or genetic factors (autism spectrum disorder). Mental health disorders affect mood, thinking, and behavior (depression, anxiety, schizophrenia). Other categories of brain disorders also include brain tumors and injuries.

According to WHO, brain health can be defined as the state of brain functioning across cognitive, sensory, social-emotional, behavioural and motor domains, allowing a person to realize their full potential over the lifetime. [source]

Prevention – Brain health

It is important to maintain good brain health because it improves mental and physical health, creates positive social and economic impacts, and leads to greater well-being while helping to advance society.

According to the WHO, brain health is determined by five different areas: [source]

Physical health

As humans, our physical health can impact our brain health in many ways because there are numerous interactions between the brain and the body. Good physical health includes maternal health and the intrauterine environment, genetic and epigenetic factors, good nutrition, and prevention of infections. We can improve our physical health by focusing on good-quality sleep, regular physical activity, and avoiding substance use.

Healthy environments

A healthy environment is especially important during developmentally sensitive stages such as early childhood, adolescence, and older age. Brain health can be negatively influenced by pollutants found in air, water, and food, as well as by neurotoxic chemicals, including heavy metals, inorganic compounds, pesticides, and organic solvents. Changing the environment is often more difficult than changing physical health, but it is still possible to make changes when necessary (for example, limiting the use of pesticides in your garden).

Safety and security

Physical safety and financial security can impact brain health throughout the life course in multiple ways. Physical safety includes stable and safe housing, as well as safety within the home and the broader community. Financial security includes the absence of stress caused by financial concerns. This means being able to afford the necessities of life, including food, housing, health care, education, and transport.

Learning and social connection

Opportunities to learn and connect with others are important for brain health throughout life. Early learning depends on caring and supportive relationships. While later-life learning including cognitive stimulation often comes through school, employment and social networks. Supporting brain health, especially in early life, often means helping people both learn and build strong social connections.

Access to quality services

At some point in our lifetime many people will still develop some brain related disease, despite best effort to minimize risk factors. Therefore it is necessary to have access to quality health and social services. Those people can then receive right diagnosis and necessary treatment.

Before You Visit

The museum contains real human remains used in medical education. We encourage quiet, respectful behaviour and emotional awareness while visiting. If you at any point feel unwell or overwhelmed, don't hesitate to step out.

Understanding how our specimens are prepared can help you appreciate the scientific and artistic processes involved in preserving the human body for education.

Plastination

The Plastination technique was created by Gunther von Hagen at the University of Heidelberg in 1977. The technique has gained worldwide acceptance and has been in use for over 40 years. It preserves anatomical specimens in such a way that they become durable, dry and odourless, while still retaining a naturalistic look. Plastinated organs are important educational tools for students.

The specimens go through a lengthy process of fixation, dehydration, forced impregnation and curing.

Step 1: Fixation

The specimen is fixed to stop putrefaction (decay). This is usually done by using formaldehyde solutions, such as formalin. Formalin fixes the tissue, which means it stops decomposition and keeps cells stable, disinfects and hardens the tissue.

Step 2: Dehydration

After the specimen is fixed and prepared, it must be dehydrated. This is done through acetone baths, which removes fluid and some fat from the organ. This is done at a cold temperature (-20°C) so that the specimen does not shrink. This process can take from 3 to 6 weeks, where the acetone needs to be changed out around three times.

For organs where fat removal is necessary, the specimen is kept in an additional acetone bath at room temperature for a period of 3–10 days. This is not done with nervous tissue, such as the brain.

Step 3: Forced Impregnation

This is the central step of plastination. It involves replacing the acetone within the tissue with silicone. A mixture of silicone and curing agent is made and the specimen is placed in the mixture. It stays in this mixture for a few days before a vacuum is applied, which boils the acetone and removes it from the organ, with silicone taking its place. This step takes 2 to 4 weeks.

Step 4: Curing

Once the silicone has sufficiently replaced the acetone, the silicone needs to be cured. This is done through gas-curing, where the specimen and the gas hardener is placed in an airtight chamber. While removing excess silicone, the specimen is in the airtight chamber for about 8 to 12 hours. Afterwards, the specimen is slowly hardened in an airtight space for months. It is fully cured in approximately 2-4 months.

Corrosion Casting

The corrosion casts are made by filling a space with a resin and removing the surrounding tissues, creating a replica of the negative space. This technique is most commonly used in vascular structures (blood vessels) but can be used for many other anatomical systems and structures.

Historical Origins

Casting like this has been around for hundreds of years. One of the earliest documented uses of this technique in anatomy was by Leonardo da Vinci around 1505–1507. Leonardo injected hot bee wax into a bovine brain ventricular system, and scraped away the surrounding tissue, leaving a cast of the ventricles.

The Process

When creating a vascular corrosion cast, a resin is injected through the specimen's vascular structures. Before casting, the vessels are flushed to ensure that they are free from blockages and blood. The vessels are fixated, ensuring that they do not expand, deform, or leak. This is done by using different solutions of chemicals, such as formaldehyde.

A special resin is then injected into the vessels at an optimal speed; too fast and the vessels may rupture, too slow and the cast may be incomplete. The resin is then left to harden.

Once hardened, the vascular tissue is placed in an alkaline solution, dissolving the tissues surrounding the resin. This leaves behind an intricate and detailed cast of the vascular system.

Wet Specimens

A wet specimen is a biological sample (animal, plant, or organ) preserved in a liquid, typically alcohol or formalin, to prevent decay and maintain its form for scientific study or display.

Preparation Process

For the preparation of wet specimens, the body parts or organs are collected and fixed properly. Then the specimens are prepared by dissection and kept in a room to dry. The passive drying usually takes one or two days.

Then the surface of the structure is coloured, to show vessels, nerves, ligaments, muscles, ducts, and glands. The vessels and nerves dry earlier than other structures, therefore the colour is applied on those before other structures.

After the colouring procedure, the specimens are placed in glass with a specific solution inside including glycerin, distilled water and formalin. Glycerin has a clearing effect on the tissue and improves the optical properties of mounting fluid. Finally the jars are covered with a lid, sealed and kept in the museum.

Ethical Guidelines

All specimens in our collection have been ethically sourced and are used solely for educational purposes. We treat all human remains with the utmost respect and dignity.

Photography may be restricted in certain areas of the museum. Please follow the guidance of museum staff and respect the wishes of other visitors who may be reflecting quietly.

If you have any questions about our ethical practices or the origins of our specimens, please don't hesitate to ask our staff.
